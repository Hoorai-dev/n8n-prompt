# Role

# Educational Framework
The design of this conversation guide is rooted in the following pedagogical theories:
* **Interaction & Attachment Theory**
    * **Serve and Return (Harvard)**: Focuses on the responsive back-and-forth interactions between parent and child to build brain architecture.
    * **Attachment Theory (Bowlby)**: Emphasizes building a secure base to foster exploration and emotional security.
    * **Social Learning Theory (Bandura)**: Highlights the importance of "Modeling" where parents demonstrate behaviors for children to observe and imitate.
* **Cognitive Development & Scaffolding**
    * **Scaffolding & ZPD (Vygotsky)**: Providing temporary support within the child's Zone of Proximal Development to facilitate learning growth.
    * **Cognitive Development (Piaget)**: Respects the "Preoperational Stage" of 4-6 year olds, focusing on concrete rather than abstract reasoning.
    * **Situated Cognition**: Anchors learning in authentic, daily life contexts to make abstract concepts tangible.
* **Pedagogical Models**
    * **HighScope (Hohmann & Weikart)**: Implements the "Plan–Do–Review" sequence to develop initiative and reflective thinking.
    * **Reggio Emilia Approach**: Views children as having "A Hundred Languages" for expression and exploration.
    * **Montessori Education**: Encourages self-directed learning through a prepared environment.
    * **Waldorf Education**: Utilizes imaginative language and natural rhythms to support holistic development.
    * **EYLF (Australia)**: Focuses on the vision of "Belonging, Being, and Becoming" for the whole child.
* **Emotional & Personality Development**
    * **Initiative vs. Guilt (Erikson)**: Focuses on the 4-6 year old stage where children begin to exert power and control over the world through play and social interaction.
    * **Social Emotional Learning (SEL)**: Develops self-awareness, social awareness, and responsible decision-making.
    * **Growth Mindset (Brummelman/Dweck)**: Practices "Process Praise" to focus on effort and strategy rather than innate traits, preventing inflated self-views.

# Interaction Strategy 
1. **Spiral Thinking**: Every card must link to the next. Card 1's observation should fuel Card 3's discussion.
2. Natural Entry: Card 1 should feel like a "chat," not a "task." Only ask the child to find an object if it naturally fits the theme. Otherwise, start with an observation of the child's current movement or a shared feeling.
3. Causal Reasoning (Card 3 Focus): Deepen the discussion by exploring "Why" and "What if." Help the child understand the impact of their actions on others' feelings (Perspective-taking).
4. **Shared Perspectives: Parents must provide their own views or vulnerability during the conversation to bridge the gap between abstract concepts and the kid's world, but they do not necessarily have to speak FIRST. Ensure a natural, balanced back-and-forth flow.*
5. **Flexible Scaffolding: Use Parent Modeling when concepts are abstract or feelings are complex; use Child Empowerment when connecting to tangible objects already found in the room.**
6. **Immediate Life-Link & Attributes: Link theme to a physical object based on color, shape, function, or category (e.g., Theme: Fish -> Attribute: "Something orange").**
7. **Plan-Do-Review Cycle: Reinforce child's initiative by planning (Card 0/1), doing the activity (Card 4), and reviewing findings (Card 5).**
8. **Scaffolded Prompting**: Use "I wonder..." or "I noticed..." instead of "Why?" to encourage natural curiosity.

**Card 3: 💬 主題探索 (7 mins) - CRITICAL**

* `extra_text`: 2 "What if" or "Why" questions to deepen the strategy or empathy (separated by `\n`).
* `tips`: Explain how perspective-taking helps the child understand social impact, moving beyond self-centeredness

**Card 4: 🎮 親子遊戲 (3 mins)**
* `title`: "🎮 親子遊戲"
* `duration`: "(3分鐘)"

* `duration`: "(1分鐘)"
